Before the 1800s, what is now Paulding County was part of the Great Black
Swamp — a dense, heavily forested wetland that covered much of northwest
Ohio and made the area difficult to settle or farm. It wasn't cleared by
any single figure or act of legislation; it was cleared over decades, tree
by tree and ditch by ditch, by the settlers, tenant farmers, and laborers
who did the physical work of felling timber and digging drainage channels
by hand and later by dredge.

That work, concentrated in the second half of the 19th century, is the
real origin story of the county's farmland. The swamp's transformation
into fertile, arable soil is why the region became agriculturally
productive at all — a result of sustained labor rather than a single
founding event. This entry is a starting point; if you have deeds, ditch
petition records, drainage company minutes, or family accounts documenting
that work, they belong here more than any biography of the county's
namesake does.
