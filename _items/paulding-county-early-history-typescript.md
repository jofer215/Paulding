## Text

The early history of Paulding County is fraught with romance,
interspersed with privations, hardships and tragedy. The County, which
is comparatively new, is not as rich in specimens of pre-historic
nature as are some of our neighbor counties; yet there are evidences
that the Mound Builders once lived in our dark and dense forests. That
there was a time in pre-historic days when huge animals of the now
extinct species roamed the wilds of Paulding County, is varied by
portions of their remains which are unearthed from time to time.
(First found was a tooth weighing 4½ lbs, on Flat Rock by a son of
John Gazat, May 1891.) Our County is rich in Indian lore and
tradition. The largest Indian Village ever located in the County was
at Charloe, beautifully situated upon the left bank of the Auglaize
River in Brown Township. It was near the center of an Indian reserve
four miles square and known as the Oquanoxas reservation. Six hundred
Indians lived here until 1820, when the reservation was sold and these
Indians, who were of the Ottawa tribe, took up their march toward the
setting sun. There were several bands of Indians along the Auglaize
and Maumee rivers. They were generally peaceable and kind to the early
white settlers except when they had indulged too freely in fire water.
No Indian tragedies of particular note occurred within the County.
General Wayne in his pursuit of the Indians passed through the county,
following the streams. During the advance of this army they came upon
a party of three Indians about one mile north of Oakwood. Two of the
Indians were shot and the third taken prisoner, from whom they
expected to get important information. A few days later a Pottawatomie
Chief was captured near Charloe. Wayne hurried on to Defiance, then
down the Maumee River to Fallen Timbers, where the Indians were
severely beaten. Then Wayne retraced his steps and marched to Ft.
Wayne, one detachment of his army passing up Flat Rock Creek, camping
a few days on what we have known as the Mossoney farm, about a mile
N.W. of Paulding.

Ohio was the first state to be carved out of the great Northwest
Territory. It was admitted into the Union in 1803. The Northwestern
part of Ohio in which Paulding County is located was the last part of
the State to be organized and settled. By an act of the State
Legislature, Ohio was divided into counties in 1820; Paulding County
was divided into 12 townships the same year. The work being done by
Alexander and Samuel Holmes. The following year the townships were
divided into sections under the direction of [surveyor's name
illegible] Riley.

Paulding County was named in honor of John Paulding, one of the
captors of the brave but unfortunate Major [André], of Revolutionary
[War] fame. His picture hangs in the Library.

The Maumee river and the military roads, cut by Wayne's Army in 1794,
became the routes and highway over which the first settlers came. The
first settlers planted their primitive homes along the banks of the
streams. The first white settlement made in the County was in
Auglaize Township near Junction, by Shadrach Hudson, in 1819. Isaac
Carey, a son-in-law of Mr. Hudson, came a few months later from Miami
County by the route which was opened to Defiance by Gen. Wayne. From
Defiance he followed the Auglaize River to his location one half mile
east of the present location of Junction. Upon this farm, on Jan. 21,
1826, was born Daniel Clark Carey, who has the credit of being the
first white child known to have been born within the limits of the
county. The Shirleys came in 1823 and the Rominis in 1825, all
settling near the present site of Junction.

Settlements along the Maumee river began in 1825 by the Hughes, Banks,
Applegates, Gordons and others. The Moss brothers, natives of England,
were the first to settle on the banks of Blue Creek, and Thomas
Wentworth and Mrs. Crain began the Flat Rock Settlement near where
Payne is located.

These early settlers were men of integrity, hardy and brave, who
uncomplainingly cracked corn for food and chased the bear, deer and
other wild animals for the same purpose. Their hardships were many but
their wants few. Their houses were built of logs, daubed with mud, with
puncheon floors, clapboard roofs, and greased paper for windows. The
clothing of the first settlers was of the simplest homespun. A small
patch of flax furnished materials for bed ticking and linen for shirts
and trousers. The wool was carded, spun, woven and fashioned into
garments by the nimble fingers of the pioneer's wife and daughters.
The early settlers were bound together with a tie of friendship best
shown in their log-rollings, husking bees, house raisings and
butchering days. They settled in a mighty forest with no tools save an
ax and maul. They had no nails, glass, plaster, lumber, brick, or
anything with which to build a shelter for their families, save forest
trees. They lived in a covered wagon until huts could be erected. The
money question gave no trouble, for their system of exchange required
little cash money. The home furnishings were mere necessities, as much
as could be brought in their covered wagon, which was often drawn by
oxen with a cow tied behind. A few chickens and possibly a hog in
crates fastened to the outside of the wagon was no uncommon sight.

In 1885 a Pioneers' Association was organized in the county. They held
annual meetings which were largely attended. Their programs consisted
of addresses, songs, pioneer papers, talks by old settlers, a
sumptuous repast and a general hand shaking. (I have had the privilege
of attending some of these meetings. The impression made on me by
these meetings will never be forgotten. I can still see in my mind's
eye some of the old men, sometimes leaning on his cane for support,
recite his early experiences of shortages of food, toil, privations
and hardships — still he was not forgetful of the joys, friendships
and amusing incidents of his pioneer life.)

The first flour mill was built at Junction in 1865. It was run by
water power, the water being furnished from the Miami Canal.

The first saw mill was built by Wm. Daggett in 1841. It was situated
about 1 mile north of Junction and received its power from the canal.
This mill was operated for 40 years.

The first school was taught by A. C. Adams in 1840. It was a log
cabin, with clapboard roof, puncheon floor, seats made of split logs,
and had a stick chimney. In 1854 a school house was built in Junction.
The first post office was established in 1842 at Junction, the second
at Arthur. The first sermon was preached by Solomon Biggs, a Methodist
Minister, in the home of John Mason. An organization was effected in
1849, and a Sunday School was soon organized.

Junction as a village was laid out in 1842 by John Mason and Nathan
Shirley. It is situated at the junction of the Wabash and Erie and the
Miami canals. In the early days canals were the great thoroughfares of
commerce, and the junction of these canals gave the new village a
flattering prospect. Daily, lines of canal boats, drawn by mules, were
established, and many passengers were transferred at this place. This
gave a prosperous business for hotels, of which there were 3. Four dry
good and grocery stores, 3 warehouses for the storage of grain, and 6
saloons, as well as many homes.

In Brown Township we find Ft. Brown, which was a small stockade that
was built at the confluence of the Big Auglaize and Little Auglaize
rivers in the year of 1812. It was occupied for a short time by a
detachment of Gen. Harrison's Army under the command of Col. [Brown?].
The first steam saw mill in the county was erected in 1852 at Charloe
by Dwight G. Blakeslee. The first post office in Brown Township was at
Ft. Brown. It was on a star route from Piqua to Defiance, and the mail
was carried on horseback once a week.

The opening of the Miami Canal changed the mail route, and post
offices were established at Charloe, Oakwood, and Melrose.

In 1837 Robert Barnhill cut a winding road up the banks of Blue Creek
and became the first settler of the township. Two years later he was
joined by Rob. [illegible], James Cunningham, and Daniel Traul. This
settlement was in the heart of a dense forest, ten miles from any
other settlement. They had to depend upon the fruits of the chase for
subsistence until their farms could be cleared and food grown. Geo.
Hakes was the first white child born in the settlement. Later Dague,
Folmer and Scott became milling [men]. Benton Twp. received its first
settlers in 1839; among the early settlers were the Bells, Northups,
Mallots, Sugars, Brown, and others.

The village of Payne, which is in both Benton and Harrison Twp., was
first called Mallottville, named from a Mr. Mallott who owned the
first store in that community. Later it was called Puckerbrush. The
name Payne was given to the post office after the coming of the
Nickel Plate Railroad.

Carryall township was the second township organized in the County and
was the most flourishing and populous. It derived its name from a
large rock in the Maumee river, just above Antwerp, which was shaped
like a boat used by the French voyageurs on the river. They called
their boats a carryall. James Hinton was the first settler in Carryall
Township, in 1827. Among other early settlers of this township were
the Applegates, Banks, Hughes, Murpheys, Graves, Pococks, and Snooks.
Carryall Twp. was the first to plant orchards, and many of the trees
grew from seed furnished by "Johnny Appleseed."

In Paulding Township the first settlement was made in 1842. Among the
early settlers are the well known names of Kimmell, Fisher, Shafer,
Barnhouse, Merchant, Savage, Latty, French, Barnes, Gregory & Hardesty.
A grist mill was built at what is now the south end of Main St. in
Paulding in 1855. Portions of the old dam may yet be seen. The first
post office in the Twp. was at Paulding, established in 1850.

The first county seat was at New Rochester, in 1839. At this time New
Rochester was the largest and most flourishing village in the County.
It was situated on the south bank of the Maumee River, about one mile
north of where Cecil now stands. It contained 30 or 40 families. It
was on a daily stage line leading from Toledo to Ft. Wayne. From here
the county seat was moved to Charloe in 1841. Charloe was laid out for
the purpose of being a county seat, and was pleasantly located on a
commanding bluff of the Auglaize River. Charloe received its name from
an Indian chief known as Charloe Peter. It was a town of four or five
hundred people. Its location was a beautiful one, its natural scenery
grand and picturesque, but like New Rochester, when deprived of its
county seat, its star of prosperity sank beneath the horizon never to
rise again.

Paulding, the third and present county seat, was laid out by George
Marsh in 1850. It became the county seat in 1851. It was in the midst
of a heavy forest, without roads, save a few winding wagon tracks. The
wild deer and bear roamed its uncleared streets and wolves howled in
its thicket-covered alleys. Many of the County officers lived in log
huts, and the first jail was made of logs. The town made but little
advancement for 30 years. In 1880 its population was 483. Stimulated
by the building of the Mackinaw railroad and the erection of several
factories, it made rapid strides until its population reached 2,000,
thereabouts to remain. There were no county buildings erected at the
first county seat, New Rochester. The only term of court held there
was in a room over Gen. H. W. Curtis' store. County offices were in
private buildings. Charloe had a courthouse erected by B. F. Hollister
at his own expense, which he had agreed to do, providing they might
secure the county seat. The building was neat for those days, being
about 30 x 40 feet, and two stories high. It was built of brick with a
stone foundation. It was the property of the county only as long as
the seat remained at Charloe. After the removal of the county seat to
Paulding, the old courthouse at Charloe became everybody's property
and nobody's property. The court room was used for all kinds of public
gatherings. The offices were used for residences, for anyone who chose
to move in. Paulding's first courthouse was built in 1852. It was a
two story frame building with offices below and a court room above. It
was a homely affair, and the court room was reached by an outside
stairway on the rear. It burned in 1868, with but few if any tears
shed over its ashes. It was replaced by a one story frame building, 30
x 40 ft. in size, and contained only a court room and 2 jury rooms. A
2-story brick building was erected for the county officials to
occupy. It served the purpose for only a few years, when it was
condemned as unfit for use, and a new court house was secured by the
circulation of a petition asking the state legislature to grant the
commissioners privilege to sell bonds to the amount of $40,000 for the
erection of a court house. The petition was presented to the State
Legislature by Hon. John L. Geyer, who was our representative from the
Paulding-Defiance district. The bill was passed and became a law that
same year, 1886. A committee visited several county seats, inspected
their court houses, and finally decided to build one using the
Adrian, Michigan Court House as a model. The laying of the corner
stone for the new court house was a gala day for Paulding. With great
pomp and ceremony, attended by a large crowd of both men and women
from town and country, the corner stone was laid Dec. 21, 1886.
Within the stone is a copper box containing a copy of the Holy Laws,
lists of the county and village officers, members of the bar
association, several pieces of coins, names of the Paulding school
board, a silver masonic square, and copies of the *Paulding Democrat*,
*Paulding Gazette*, and *Antwerp Argus*. The building was completed
and accepted by the Commissioners May 24, 1888, at a total cost of
building and furnishings of $42,524.29.

Paulding County's first jail was a small brick building erected at
Charloe in 1842. This jail was seldom ever occupied during these
early pioneer days. The 2nd jail was built on the southeast corner of
the court yard in Paulding. It was a log building made with double
walls, and some of the cells were lined with heavy plates of sheet
iron. In spite of the extra precautions, escapes from jail were
frequent. A new and modern jail was erected in 1876 (our present
jail). The first 2 sheriffs were appointed, Andrew Smith and Ephiam
Burwell. A county home for our homeless was established at an early
date, a log structure (a more modern building was erected in 1884),
present location.

The first Rep. in our State Legislature was Hon. John W. Ayres. The
first State Senator from the County was Hon. P. W. Hardesty (seven or
eight counties in the Senatorial district).

*[The road to] Ft. Wayne, completed [18]65, with the usual
opposition.* Prior to 1842, before the [canal] became operative, the
mode of travel was on foot or on horseback, and the means of
transporting goods and produce was by flat boats on the rivers. The
roads were often impassable, yet the fight against railroad and
improved highways were bitterly fought by many of the early settlers.

Although the county was new and hardships great, its citizens were not
heedless to the call to arms when the country was stirred by the fall
of Ft. Sumter in 1861. It aroused the pulse of the pioneer, whose
pulse beat in unison with that of the patriotic North. A company known
as Co. G. was organized to become a part of the 14th regiment of
Ohio's volunteer infantry. It was recruited from all parts of the
county, with headquarters at Antwerp. The commissioned officers were
John S. Snook, Capt.; Alfred M. Russell, 1st Lieut.; and John Corson,
2nd Lieut. All through the war Paulding Co. was not lacking in its
patriotism and willingness to do its part in preserving the union.

In the spring of 1887 armed troops were sent into the county to quell
a so-called lawless mob, who felt that the reservoir which supplied
the Wabash & Erie Canal with water had served its purpose, and, in an
effort to get rid of the so-called menace, when the State Legislature
refused to grant its abandonment, bodies of men proceeded to blow up
the banks and allow the escape of the water. A band of about 200 men,
masked, marched on the guards, captured them, and proceeded to
dynamite and dig trenches through the banks which would allow it to
drain. April 25, 1887, locks were burned and the keeper's house
burned; armed reinforcements arrived and a camp of guards was
established. This camp was named Camp Dynamite. Order was restored. A
Gatling gun which was in possession of the guards attracted much
attention. Hostilities ceased and the camp became a resort for sight
seers. Troops were soon recalled, and Gov. Foraker visited the scene
of the recent trouble and recommended the abandonment of the
reservoir, which he said was just cause for grievances.

The early churches were served by circuit riders, the first of whom
was Rev. E. C. Gavitt; meetings were held in the homes or school
houses.

The first doctor known to be in the county was Dr. Richard Allison,
who accompanied Gen. Wayne in his memorable march through the county.
Some of our pioneer physicians were Dr. John Kingrey of Brown
Township, Dr. Duval B. Cooper of Charloe, Dr. Marcellus of Junction,
and Dr. Woodcock of Antwerp. Dr. Kingrey was a farmer and shoemaker as
well as a physician. The following entry on his book may be of
interest:

> Dec. [xx]th, A.D. 1845.
>
> Making one pr. of shoes 62½¢ — To 1 bu. of turnips, 10¢ — To 1 bu.
> of potatoes, 25¢. To medical attention $1.00. Total $1.97½.

Dr. H. M. Ayers located at Charloe in the early sixties, was
thoroughly educated in the profession, and began a reform in
medicine. He was a medical graduate. He later became a partner with
his daughter, Dr. Lillie Ayres, who was a graduate of the Cincinnati
Medical College. Lillie Ayres married Dr. Martin; her home remains in
Paulding.

The first newspaper in the County was the *Age of Progress*, published
by P. W. Hardesty in 1853. It was sold in a few months to the Hon. A.
S. Latty, who named his paper the *Democrat*. After two years this
paper was sold to J. D. Baker and became the *Republican*. The
*Republican* lived a year. The equipment was bought by Ezra J. Smith,
who began publishing the *Eagle*. In 1859 the *Eagle* folded its
pinions and sank to rest. The stock was purchased by S. R. Brown, who
published its valedictory number four years later. Next came the
*Paulding Press*, which was soon renamed the *Rural Ohioan*. Then came
the *Plain Dealer*, published by Wesley A. Savage, June 30, 1870. The
*Plain Dealer's* obituary appeared 3 years later. In 1869 Hon. Joseph
Cable began publishing the *Review* in Antwerp. He moved his enterprise
to Paulding. Three years later he sold out to Hammond & DeWitt, who
published the *Journal*. Two years later the *Journal* became the
property of Mr. [illegible], who founded the present *Democrat* paper.

What is now the *Paulding Republican* was founded in Antwerp, April
1886, by Willis E. Osborn. It was called the *Gazette*. It too had
many owners and publishers. Mr. Durfey being the owner who changed the
name to the *Paulding Co. Republican*. Mr. Durfey sold the paper to
Mr. J. E. Ross, the present publisher, about 1890. It was later sold
to [illegible] Miller, and then in 194[?] consolidated with the
*Democrat* paper, called the *Paulding Progress*.

The first undertaker in the county was [Mr.] Schilb. He was located at
Antwerp. After his death, Mrs. Schilb continued the business — our
first woman undertaker in the state.

Paulding now has a million dollar sugar [beet] factory, a Carnegie
Library, and has an up-to-date high school and auditorium. The new
[wing] and repairs on the old building [cost] approximately $220,000.

Paulding merchants are progressive and up to date.

Paulding is well churched. There are 6 churches for white and 2 for
colored people. There is an Armory, in which the National Guards are
drilled and trained. All the main streets are either paved or hard
surfaced. Being located in a rich farming community, it supports
modern elevators for handling grain, a grist mill, and a seed store
that does foreign shipping, as well as caring for home needs.

Paul Eichling, a farmer living west of the town, developed a variety
of hybrid corn seed which is winning not only national but foreign
recognition.
