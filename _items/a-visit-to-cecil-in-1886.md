*From the Paulding Democrat of April 8, 1886:*

## CECIL CHIMES

Twenty years ago the branches of the majestic elm waved in the unbroken
forest where now stands the village of Cecil. The origin of its location
is that the Paulding Furnace having been built on the canal, and the
proprietors wishing a railroad communication nearer than Emerald, they
prevailed upon the Wabash [Railroad] Company to give them a station as
near a point as possible on that road, which was done in the year of
1867, and Martin N. Utley was appointed the agent.

Mr. Utley also embarked in the mercantile business and has been a moving
spirit of the town since that date. His first business room was a log
cabin, which was superseded in a short time by a neat frame, which is
now occupied by William Wallace as a hotel and saloon, Mr. Utley having
subsequently erected a large two-story frame where he is now the
proprietor of as fine a general store as the county affords.

W. H. Robertson and Russell Randolph are the other merchants and both
have good rooms and fine stores.

The principal factory of the place is the stave factory of the Beugnot
Bros. & Co., and was put in operation in 1878. The factory furnishes
employment to about 35 hands and has an average cutting capacity of
about 24,000 staves per day. The firm also has 560 acres of land in
Sections 35 and 36 of Crane Township from which they get stave bolts for
their factory. Upon this land they have located a sawmill and
manufacture lumber extensively, and have a switch running from their
mill to the Mackinaw* over which they ship this lumber to market. One
hundred and 50 acres of their land is improved and they are tiling and
propose to farm the same and more as fast as it is cleared. The firm
controls about 100 hands in their various branches of business in the
county.

Cecil has two church edifices, one Catholic and one Presbyterian, both
frame. The Presbyterian was erected in 1878 and the Catholic in 1880.
The United Brethren denomination talk of erecting a church in the coming
summer.

Cecil has a nice brick Town Hall erected in 1880. It is a two-story
building about 24 feet wide by 40 feet in length.

The school house is a neat two-story brick erected in 1879 and is a very
substantial and beautiful building. The spring term will close for a
month's vacation on Friday, April 9th.

Cecil was incorporated in 1879, and has now about 400 inhabitants.

H. M. Simpson is the proprietor of a tile factory and sawmill and does a
good business.

Coe Robison is the only tonsorial artist** in the place. He has neat
rooms and is ever ready to wait upon all customers.

John Kain has lately opened out the only meat market of the place. He
will run a wagon to Sherwood and will do a good business. Success, John.

Allen Boyer has lately opened a new shoe shop. Charlie Leaders and Ed
Williams, two first class workmen, are in his employ. We trust that Mr.
Boyer will receive good patronage.

A large frame hotel was erected last fall by Orsemus Bartholomew
Chesbrough, who has lately furnished it nicely and will soon open it to
the traveling public.

John Mast is the only blacksmith in the place. He is a good workman and
does a large business.

The genial John White settled near Cecil as a shiptimberman about 20
years ago. He is the present proprietor of a billiard hall and lunch
room on south side of the Wabash track and invites all to come and see.

Delphos Guerin has a fine grocery store and sells piles of goods.

The Wabash depot is a neat, substantial frame and was built in 1879.
T. A. Wesley, the genial passenger and freight agent, took charge of the
depot about three years ago and is well liked by all. He informs us that
the receipts of the freight office is $4,000 per month and the passenger
receipts about $400.

Owing to the present prospect of the Mackinaw being built northward this
season, a boom for Cecil is bound to come and in view of this it would
be an excellent point for a flouring mill, in fact one of the best in
the county; a good drug store and hardware store are also much needed as
there are none in the town.

Take it all in all Cecil certainly has a bright prospect of becoming a
prosperous and thriving village, situated as it is in the midst of a
fine farming country and with, when the Mackinaw is completed, the best
railroad facilities of any place in the county. Success to the place and
to the good people there.

*The Mackinaw line, a nickname for the Cincinnati, Jackson & Mackinaw
Railroad. It later became the Cincinnati Northern and eventually the New
York Central.

**Tonsorial artist is another term for barber.
