*The following story was written about 1926. The typed page was marked at
the bottom "Property of N.E. Harper, Paulding Ohio 1928." The story and
photograph were acquired from the Center for Archival Collections at
Bowling Green State University.*

## TO LOVERS OF THE ANTIQUE:

Memories of the cabin days, with the plank kitchen, the Sycamore gum
smoke house, the deer horns, and powder horn. Paulding County still holds
one such unmatchable scene. The story follows:

It was on a raw, wet March day that a hoary bearded large man entered the
writer's store and said, "show me the heaviest cow hide shoe you have,
size eleven." Looking me straight in the eye he said, "Don't you know me
anymore? My name is Beck. Lewis Beck, the old pioneer of Bluecreek
Township."

I said, "Sure, you are the man that lived neighbor to Jim Barnhill."

"Yes, and I built my cabin in 1874 and am still living in it and expect
to die in it. It is going to be a tight race for she is bulging out on
one side but I have some strong props on her."

I procured the shoes for him; he pulled a cold bare foot out of a thin
wet shoe, stating that he never had any use for socks. But after some
strong argument I induced him to put on a pair of heavy socks. The deal
being made he wore the new outfit home.

During the fitting of the shoes, the conversation ran something like
this:

"How old are you now, Mr. Beck?"

"I am 78."

"Did you ever shoot any deer?"

"I got my share."

"Do you still have your old deer rifle?"

"Yes, sir."

"Any deer horns hanging around your cabin?"

"Plenty of them. I am keeping them for the children. My family is raised
and gone and I am alone."

I told him I was coming over some day to take pictures of him and his
place. I thought strange that he made no reply and after talking it over
with one of his old neighbors who resides in Paulding, on the following
October we concluded to pay him a brief visit and get some snap shots.
Fortunately for us on our arrival at the home we found his married
daughter there sweeping up the rag carpets and adding new coats of white
wash to the rooms, etc.

We received a hearty welcome and soon had a picture of Mr. Beck with deer
horns and rifle in hands standing with his daughter in front of the cabin
door. In the next picture he is standing with rifle in hand and the
little dog at his feet. We then took a picture of Mr. Beck and Mr.
Grooms standing by the plank kitchen.

We then went around the house to the old smoke house there Mr. Beck
removed his hat and I believe this to be one of the rarest antique
pictures in existence.

Mr. Beck remarked, "I wish I had some of the good hams I had stole out of
that gum."

A peek into the living room revealed the old cook stove with the two long
hind legs, the low down ceiling logs and plank upper floor covered with
pure white wash, the cozy reading corner with lamp and easy chair. Mr.
Beck can entertain you on any subject you wish to discuss from hunting
wild bees and honey to a trip to the White House and meeting the
President.

The old log stable is a wreck, but who cares; the logs are there, he
needs no horse. The old open well is standing there full of water and
uncovered. It is good enough. The tall pear trees in the front yard are
loaded with fruit and when the fruit strikes the ground the juice flies.
Oh, My! The country store is a mile and half away where he frequently
walks for his supplies and to exchange notes with neighbors.

Mr. Beck is now past eighty years of age and going strong and
occasionally adds a new log prop to the east side of his cabin. We trust
that this little sketch in no way reflects unkindness to this splendid
old pioneer or his loyal family, friends, or relation.

The October haze is gathering in the west and the pink and purple are
blending for a perfect sunset. The shadows are lengthening. The mist is
hanging over the silent meadows; the goldenrod is nodding along the
highway and we are speeding home.

*Hand-written at the bottom of the page: Lewis Beck died in September
1928, aged 81 years. The old cabin has fallen.*
