Andrew Carnegie initially refused Paulding County's request for a
library grant, citing insufficient population — until the Library
Association proposed county-wide service through book stations, at
which point he granted $40,000 (county commissioners A.E. Blakeslee,
S.O. Jackson, and J.L. McClure matched it with a continuing maintenance
fund equal to 10% of the grant). The site — four lots on South Main
Street, part of an 1835 congressional patent deed to George Marsh, who
platted the Village of Paulding in 1850 — was donated by Rebecca S.
Latty, widow of A.S. Latty, who had acquired the lots by 1854; the
grounds had also served as the Latty family's private cemetery before
the bodies were exhumed and relocated.

The Columbus architectural firm Howard & Merriam (which had also
designed the first U.S. presidential library, the Rutherford B. Hayes
Library in Fremont, Ohio) designed the building. Construction began in
1914 — the Willing Brothers of Bellevue, Ohio handled general
construction, Bashore Brothers of Paulding the heating, plumbing, and
lighting — and the library opened to the public on March 3, 1916, with
an initial collection of 4,000 books and a staff of three (librarian
Ella Smith, assistant Vivian Jackson, and shipping clerk A.V.R. Mott).
The first patron to check out a book, on a subject fittingly close to
the county's roots, was A.A. Abuer — a book about agriculture.
