Worstville — "practically a ghost town" by 1987, with only 35-40
residents — grew up alongside the Nickel Plate Railroad (built 1881)
a few miles southwest of Paulding, near Payne and Briceton. At its
peak in the late 19th/early 20th century it had a Methodist church,
school, hotel, grocery store, post office, depot, and (per the Center
for Ghost Town Research in Ohio) some saloons, though interviewed
residents recalled no knowledge of any. Its main industry was making
barrel staves.

Charlie Cline, 81 at the time of the article and still farming at
Worstville's south edge, recalled one dramatic night as a young boy:
his father loaded him and his brother into a buggy to watch a grain
elevator burn down (there was no nearby fire department at the time);
it was later rebuilt.
