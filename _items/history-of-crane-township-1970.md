*This history of Crane Township and Cecil was taken from an article
written by Francis Minck of Cecil for the 1970 Paulding County
Sesquicentennial, and reprinted in the historical society's newsletter
ahead of Crane Township's 2025 bicentennial.*

The Wabash Canal was completed in 1843 and the Miami-Erie Canal was
completed in 1845. The Toledo, Wabash & Western Railroad was completed
in 1855. The Paulding-Cecil Railroad extended from Paulding to Cecil,
and was a narrow gauge. The first train ran over this road in September
1880.

The heavy growth of timber in this area invited several kinds of
manufacturing establishments; one was a furnace for smelting of iron
ore. It was the Paulding Iron Company, Ltd. It was located in Section 25
on the canal south of Cecil. The furnace was built in 1864-65, burnt
down in 1875 and rebuilt in 1876. Lake Superior region ore was used and
was brought by ships to Toledo then brought up the canal by boat. The
ore cost was $10 per ton. The furnace burned charcoal which was the
hardwood of the territory and burned in kilns, several being just south
of the Cecil Bridge. Its cost was 7-1/2 cents a bushel. Limestone was
used as flux, which came from Section 17 in Defiance Township at $1.50
per perch at the furnace. They poured about 11 tons of gray iron in 24
hours. The finished product sold from $40 to $44 a ton.

The village of Cecil was laid out April 1, 1871 by M. N. Utley, who ran
a store in Cecil. At this time, there were two stave mills and a steam
sawmill.

After the county seat was moved from New Rochester, this village became
the thriving center of business, with the two railroads, the Wabash and
the Cincinnati Northern (which are now the N&W and the Penn Central).
The Wabash ran eight passenger trains daily as it was the main route
between Toledo and St. Louis; and the CN ran six passenger trains daily.

There were two hotels, two doctors, two blacksmith shops and two livery
stables. There were also three barber shops, three general stores and a
hardware and implement store, a tile mill and a grain, hay and seed
business. There were also three churches and a very good school.

There were two telephone companies in Cecil. The Home Telephone Company
and the Cecil Mutual. The telephone lines were on both sides of the road
and many people had both phones so they could talk to their neighbors.
The Home Telephone Company, built in 1904, was located upstairs over the
hardware store until the building burned January 20, 1918. After this
the Home Telephone Co. merged with the Cecil Mutual Co., which was
located in the Montroy residence. They also had a millinery shop in
their home.

The only other village in Crane Township, Knoxdale, was located four
miles west of Cecil along the Wabash R.R. with a few houses, a general
store and a depot.

Today, the remaining places of business in Cecil are: Denver's Market,
Hart's Auto Parts, Cecil Grain Co. and the Lone Tower Station. There are
also three churches and a Post Office. In the Township are located The
General Portland Cement Co., the France Stone Co., and Schwerman
Trucking Company. Farming still remains one of the principle means of
livelihood in the Township.
