*From the Progress of Sept. 24, 1997. By Nancy Whitaker, Feature Writer.*

## Jail tales from the days when every community had a hoosegow

PAULDING COUNTY IS RICH in history. If walls could talk ... oh what our
ears would hear! It was surprising to learn that the old Latty jail is
still intact although no one can remember when it was used last. The
former jail house is located at the rear of the current town hall. Mayor
Tom Sinn unlocked the hall and the jail, to reveal what jails were like in
the 1800s.

The Latty jail still has the original bars across the windows, two
holding cells, cots which fold up and hook to the walls, iron bars at
each end and some type of metal storage container at one end. (Perhaps
for toileting purposes?)

The two cells are actually four feet wide and eight feet long. They are
also seven feet high. The floor, ceiling and sides are made of boiler
plate or steel. The metal partitions between the cells are also steel
plated with solid bars in the rear. The doors to the cells are iron-made
bars. Actually, it seems hard to believe that anyone ever managed to
escape from this jail.

The Village of Latty, located west of U.S. 127 and the south side of Ohio
613, was laid out in 1882 by A.S. Latty and Wrexham Lewis. Both of these
gentlemen owned the land on which Latty was platted. The village of Latty
was incorporated in 1883 and became a lively little town with hotels,
grocery stores, a stave factory, a couple of taverns, a school and two
churches. Latty was also located on the Mackinac and Nickel Plate
Railroad. In the year 1890, Latty had a population of 594 people. In the
1990 census, the population was approximately 205.

The first village officials of Latty were George Cook, mayor; Charles
Ogen, clerk; Simeon E. Ropp, treasurer; and Charles Paulus, the first
marshal.

A lot of the villages at this time had their own jails and their own
judges, better known as The Mayor. Mayor's Court in Latty dated back to
1884, but Tom Sinn says that he has been Mayor of Latty for 12 years and
does not remember when the last Mayor's Court was held.

Well, Latty, like most of the towns of this time period, had its own
jail. And, yes, it was used. What crimes did people commit in the late
1800s? What was the punishment, and how much was the fine? Wherever there
was a railroad crossing and a tavern, you can bet there were good times!
There were no vehicles at this time, so there were no DUIs. But, public
intoxication was a crime! Listed under the criminal dockets, people who
were arrested for being drunk were fined one dollar and costs. Also, they
were thrown in jail and had to pay a total of 60 cents for three meals.
The fee for intoxication did go up to $5.

Another "no-no" was keeping a saloon open past certain hours. One poor
man had to post $50 bail and then was fined $5. These may seem like cheap
fines, but that was a lot of money in those days! Then, of course, there
was making spirits. You could say they were medicinal but not if you made
over a gallon.

In 1885, a person stole a pair of rubber boots. He was caught and had to
spend 30 days in "the clink." His total fines and costs were $5.55.
Another incident was the stealing of two pair of pants, a coat, a vest,
and some underwear. A citizen filed the complaint, but when the marshal
did a search of the party in question, he couldn't find anything.

In the late 1800s, fighting was common. Not just the men fought and
argued, so did the women. You could wind up in jail for pointing a toy
pistol at someone. Women were supposed to conduct themselves in a
becoming manner. A lady was put in jail for using profane language. She
didn't have enough money to pay her fine, so she went to jail. But, a man
could beat his wife and only get a $1 fine. And there was another case
when a wife beat her husband. Another incident reported was a girl
laughing and acting up in church. She had to go to jail for 30 days!

Yes, the jails were busy, but in most cases, the defendant went to court
the next day. The little jail cells were not a place folks liked to be.
One common thing young men enjoyed was jumping on a train. But, if they
got caught, they had to pay a fine of $1. You could do a lot of different
annoyances, from shooting off firecrackers to trespassing, and receive
the dollar fine. One time a man stole a pig. By the time the pig was
discovered to be missing it seems that the poor pig was the main entree
on the supper table. The pig's owner couldn't prove the pig had been his.
So, he was out one pig.

What a time the late 1800s must have been! Since that time, jails have
improved, and now we see a lot more violent crimes. The Latty Jail, no
doubt, saw a lot of action in those days. But, it must have been built to
last, for it is still standing.

Most of the villages did indeed have their own jails, but if a big crime
were committed, the offender was sent to the county jail.

If you lived in this time period, you had better watch your P's and Q's.
The little Latty jail is a unique piece of history, still standing as it
had been yesterday. Adding to the scene was the eerie creaking of the
cell doors. One of my main questions was, "Wonder what they had to eat?"
Was it bread and water?

Congratulations to the Village of Latty for preserving this piece of
history. But, I'm glad I'm not in the clink.
