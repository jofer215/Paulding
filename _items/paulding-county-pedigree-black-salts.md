*From the Progress of April 15, 1998. By Caroline Zimmerman.*

One of the pioneer industries of Paulding Co. was the manufacture of
black salts. Ashes were gathered into barrels in which holes had been
drilled near the bottom. Water was poured into the ashes making a strong
lye. This lye was boiled down until black crystals or "black salts" were
formed.

Such an "ashery" was started on small scale on the creek bank at the east
edge of Payne. In the early days of its history, it consisted of only a
furnace and a large boiler and vat in a small shed, and a few ash
hoppers. At this time only black salts were made. Originally operated by
David Malott, it was sold to Michael Englehart, brother to the owner of
the Paulding ashery. Improved machinery was installed for making potash
from the lye.

*(This ashery and its "Bandy" Englehart proprietor are also described
from a different angle in this archive's ["Paulding County's Past"]({{ "/archive/paulding-countys-past-don-price/" | relative_url }})
entry, under "The Potassium Factory.")*

The lye was boiled down to a thick syrup when it was dipped into cooking
pans. At this stage it had a red flame-like appearance. When cold, it was
very hard and heavy and packed into barrels for shipment and was so heavy
three men were required to load it on a wagon.

The potash was then sold as high as $28 a barrel. Potash being a
vegetable alkali in an impure state, procured from the ashes of plants
(tree vegetation).

It was lucrative for the local children to sell the ashes accumulated in
the ash barrel or box at homes in the area, because everyone burned wood.
Also, the "Ash Man" would make rounds to buy ashes from 3 cents to 8
cents a basket. Ashers were gathered from near and far, from saw mills,
from burned log heaps, from factories, stores and even from some homes.

And "ashery" was a long, low and ramshackle building, which disappeared
with the timber industry.

In a dozen short years, the heavily forested acres of Benton and
Harrison Townships had been laid bare like a "plucked chicken," leaving
the blackened stumps like "pinfeathers of a barred rock rooster."

The major sources of income for the early settlers were products
produced from Paulding County timber. The manufacture of black salts was
one product of days gone by.

The following poem was taken from the centennial souvenir booklet "The
Progress of Paulding County 1839-1939":

## PAYNE

First they called it Flatrock city,
&nbsp;&nbsp;But this did not remain
For it was changed to Malottville.
&nbsp;&nbsp;And then they called it Payne.

Her lands were rich and seeds would grow
&nbsp;&nbsp;And soon the farmers came,
And now the stock years of the town
&nbsp;&nbsp;Have reached the point of fame.

It ever was a thriving town,
&nbsp;&nbsp;A fitting place to be;
That is the reason Payne has grown
&nbsp;&nbsp;Says county history.

The first export cargo of the county was said to have been 25 raccoon
skins, eight deer pelts, 10 pounds of dried ginseng and a pair of venison
hams, loaded into a canoe by a squatter at some point in the northern
part of Emerald Township, and shipped down the Maumee to Defiance. The
cargo was valued at about $25 or $30, according to the 1892 *Historical
Atlas of Paulding County, Ohio.*
