*From the Paulding County Republican, Jan. 19, 1922:*

Although the Wabash canal was in operation at least two years earlier
than the Miami & Erie, it never seemed to support quite so many towns,
even though the two had nearly the same amount of mileage in the county.

This lack of towns has been accounted for on two leading points.

One was that the Wabash railroad followed closely on the heels of the
canal. Even while the canal was being dug, there was talk of the
railroad. This had a tendency to keep down the canal traffic. On the
other hand, it was not so far to market for the products along the
Wabash. A goodly portion of the timber found ready sale close to home,
necessitating but short hauls.

During the latter days of the great civil strife [the Civil War], capital
became interested in two large enterprises along this canal. One of them
was located in Section 23, Crane Township, and the other a half mile
east of Antwerp. They were blast furnaces and were placed in operation
in 1864-65.

In those days, the secret of melting iron ore with coal had not yet been
discovered. They thought it all had to be done with charcoal. Charcoal
being made from wood, it was decided that the cheapest way to get the
wood and ore at the same place was to bring the ore to the wood. This
proceeding was adhered to from 1845 to 1887. During the latter year, the
furnace [Paulding Furnace] in Crane Township was discontinued. The one at
Antwerp [Antwerp Furnace] had ceased operations a couple of years before.

During the time these plants were in operation they furnished employment
for several hundred men. There was an almost constant stream of ore
boats coming down from the Lake Superior region. The iron was shipped to
the eastern mills to be converted into the finished product.

Folks who were early enough on the scene to witness the operation of
these two enterprises describe them as being a couple of wonderful
industries for their day. They averaged a run of about eight months out
of every 12. About 2,500 tons of iron were produced by each plant per
year. Most of it was what was designated as pig iron, but a portion of
the time they made a better grade than this.

On account of these flourishing enterprises, it was not necessary to
have many towns along the Wabash.

Taking it from Junction there was no settlement worthy of the name for
five or six miles, when you came to Reid's Post Office, or Tate's
Landing, as it was more commonly called. It was a flourishing little
place and was scattered along the canal banks for a distance of nearly a
quarter of a mile, despite the fact that Emerald Station, on the
railroad, was only a short distance away.

Going toward the west there were no more towns until you are to the
Paulding Furnace, a mile and a half south of where the town of Cecil is
now.

From there on west to the state line there was no town except Antwerp,
which has always been a flourishing place. It was laid out when the
canal was put through. It had the advantage of the others as it was on
both the canal and the railroad.

That portion of the canal which was in Indiana was entirely abandoned
about the year 1870. This rendered almost useless that portion between
the state line and Junction. This was abandoned by an act of the
legislature in 1888.

Before it was finally done away with, however, there was a long fight,
with the state authorities on one side and a number of land owners on
the other. Considerable enmity was engendered. The bulkheads of Six Mile
Reservoir in Carryall Township was blown up and much of the water let
out. Most of the locks were demolished, thus rendering the canal
useless. A company of state militia was sent into the county to quell
what was termed the lawless element. Most of the dynamiting had been
done before the arrival of the soldiers and they did not remain long.

The fight over the abandoning of the reservoir and canal [the "Reservoir
War"] make one of the most interesting chapters in the history of
Paulding County. It fills one with something akin to pride when
listening to a recital of the doings of the swarthy pioneers who took
the execution of their rights into their own hands and made possible the
cultivation of not only all of the lands within the banks of the
reservoir, but several hundred acres that were practically useless on
account of backwaters.
